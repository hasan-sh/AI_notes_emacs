1 Statistics

Statistic is the science of data. Through statistics we analyze certain data taken from a certain population, and that subsequently allows us to draw conclusions.

  • Population is a term denoting to the domain of which the census in the collected data are
  • Census is the "objects" in the population; objects= humans, animals tables etc.

In stats, we usually take a sample data (i.e. a subset of the population), and then make analysis on this sample. Moreover, it is important that the sample is collected unbiasedly.

2 Collecting sample data

There are different sampling methods for the data:

  • The easiest is voluntary response; however this could be biased since subjects decide whether to participate.
  • Random sample each has an equal prob to be selected
  • Simple Random sample selects samples of participants randomly from a set of n participants. This is unbiased, but cumbersome as well (i.e. if the data is huge)
  • Systematic sampling: selects the k-th member in a population, after a starting point (this is not reliable, since you can manipulate where to start)
  • Stratified sampling: a better method. It divides the population into subgroups and then selects x members from each subgroup which would form a sample.
  • Cluster sampling: we have clusters from the population and then select some members from clusters randomly.
  • Convenience sampling: easily available results (biased and not very reliable), however this is okay for quick sampling method, e.g. send a survey to start a pilot.

There are also different types of study in statistics:

  1. Observation: in which the characteristics of subjects are observed; without modifying the subject
  2. Experiment: some subject treatment;
    1. control and treatment group; single-blind (in which the subjects are not aware of the placebo) or double-blind (the researcher and subjects do not know who took placebo)
    2. , this way we try to not have any biased effect.

Note the parameters denote to the characteristics of the population, whereas statistics about the characteristics of the samples

2.1 Data types

When analyze data, we have two types:

  1. Qualitative: names or labels that represent counts or measurement
    1. Nominal: names, labels, categories (no ordering as high or low)
    2. Ordinal: categories that can be ordered; grades A,B,C and D that a student is given. Note this ordering does not indicate any computational significance in the differences between them (i.e. A is better than B, but B - A is meaningless.)
  2. Quantitative: numbers represent counts or measurements. Two types: Interval (without zero; ie starting point) and Ratio (with zero)
    1. Discrete set of possible values is countable (e.g. # of siblings). Thus this denotes to things that are finite. Moreover, the event can have an infinite number of values, but as far as the observed variable is countable, it is discrete; e.g. I drink fewer cola cans than my cousin.
    2. Continuous: set of possible values is uncountable (e.g. weight of x sibling). Thus this denotes to things that are infinite. Meaning we cannot find the exact number of a given event; e.g. CocaCola company possibly produces between 0-100 kg of cola each hour.

Moreover, data can also be:

  • interval level of measurement; if the data can be ordered and do NOT contain a 0. Meaning the data can have a meaningful difference between them. E.g. years 1990-2000 have a difference of 10 years (which is meaningful), they can be ordered, and they do not start from 0 (i.e. 0 is arbitrary and does/can not represent a starting point of time; i.e. "no time")
  • on the contrast, ratio level of measurement; if the data can be ordered and DO contain a 0. Meaning the data can have a meaningful difference and ratio between them. E.g. volume of beer in a bottle is 0.4l for BottleA and 0.8l for BottleB. We can find the difference, and even say BottleA has twice as much beer as BottleB, and 0 represent "no beer".

We can summarize data numerically or graphically. In a numerical way, we calculate, e.g., the mean or standard deviation. Although, this summary is useful, but a graph that shows the characteristics of the data distribution enhances the ability to read and understand the data. Moreover, the choice depends on the research question.

2.2 Graphical summaries

Here are some types of graphs to summarize data:

  • Frequency distribution: table which shows the n of occurences of each category.
    • this can be cumulative frequencies as well
  • Bar chart: frequency or qualitative data.
  • Pareto bar chart: the categories are order in respect to their frequencies (applicable for nominal data; since in ordinal, there might be a key of 9 with frequency more than that of 4)
  • Histogram: bar areas are proportional to frequency in respect to an interval. And is used for quantitative data.

Note when comparing data, it's preferred that the same scale is used.

2.3 Describe data

Location of the data distribution (i.e. position on the x axis). Moreover, we can read the dispersion (spread/variation) of the dataset

Sometimes we cannot decide where the location and dispersion are, therefore we can use numerical summaries.

  • Measure of center: at the center or middle of a dataset, different measures of center:
    • mean: is the average of a dataset of n numbers. Not robust, because it depends on the extreme values in a dataset (e.g. dataset is 1,3,100, the mean will be far from 1 and 3)
      • population mean: (greak letter μ) sum all data values and divide the size of the population.(mostly cannot be calculated, since the size is not always available)
      • sample mean: sum data values and divide by the size of the sample (estimate)
    • median: the middle value of the data set (after sorting). This measure is robust and not affected by extreme values (e.g. dataset is 1,100,3, the median here is 3 after sorting)
      • if n of dataset is even, the median is the average of the two numbers in the middle.
    • mode: the data value that occurs with the highest frequency.
      • Moreover, a dataset with 1 mode is called unimodal, with 2 is bimodal, and with >2 multimodal. This can be the title of a graph using any of them.

We can also measure the variation of a data set. One important thing is to scale the graphs in order to have a clearer visualization of the variation.

But how do we quantify the variation measure? By means of Standard Deviation. SD measures how much the values deviates from the sample means. Variance SD is the sd2.

Another measure, is the range(min, max), but this is not robust.

Moreover, we can measure the location and dispersion by percentiles. Percentile Pi: i% of data values is smaller than Pi and (100-i)% is larger.

Special percentiles: quartiles: Q1=P25, Q2=P50=median, and Q3=P75. (in R use summary(data) and quantile(data)). The spread is then calculated by the difference: Q3 - Q1.

This summary is called 5-number summary: give the min, quartiles and max.
