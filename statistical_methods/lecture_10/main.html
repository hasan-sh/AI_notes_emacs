1 Goodness of Fit

The main idea is to check whether the observed expected frequencies (which is calculated by some observed frequencies) fit the claimed distribution! Take the example of M&Ms colors which are expected to be in the given percentages.

First we calculate the expected frequencies based on the claimed distribution then hypothesize: H0: frequency counts agree with the claimed distribution H1: frequency counts do not agree with the claimed distribution The Ei is calculated by n * pi where p is the probability of element under the i index. 

Requirement: E_i >= 5; all expected frequencies are at least 5.

If the requirement is met, we compute the test statistic: χ2 = ∑((Oi - Ei)2 / Ei ~ χ2(k-1) under the H0 (df is k-1 where k is the number of categories.)

We reject the H0 when we have large numbers of χ2 score:

  • critical value: reject H0 if χ2 > χ2(k-1,a) where the latter is from Table 4
  • P-value: reject H0 if P(X2 >= χ2) < α
the goodness-of-fit is always right-tailed test; because we want to reject the H_0 
if the difference between the expected and observed frequencies is large.

2 Contingency Tables

Frequency counts of categorical data that correspond to two different variables. We have rows (r categories) and columns (c categories). Oij is = the observed value under the i-th row and j-th column.

First there are two requirements:

  1. 2*2 tables all expected frequencies >= 5
  2. larger tables all Eij >= 1 and 80% of Eij >= 5

2.1 independence

The H0: row and column variables are independent and H1 row and column variables are dependent

The test statistic is similar to the one of Goodness of Fit, but with the corresponding row and column of each expected and observed frequency. This has (approx.) a distribution of χ2((r-1)*(c-1)) under the H0

In the null hypothesis we want to hypothesize for large values. Thus the same of Goodness-of-fit.

Under the H0, we can compute Eij by: /(i-th row total)*(j-th column total) / all total (i.e. n).

If the requirements are met, we can compute observed value (i.e. test statistic) and compare it with the critical value (under χ2((r-1)(c-1),α) distribution) and check whether it is withing the critical region (see below)

We reject the H0 when we have large numbers of χ2 score:

  • critical value: reject H0 if χ2 > χ2((r-1)(c-1),α) where the latter is from Table 4
  • P-value: reject H0 if P(X2 >= χ2) < α

2.2 Homogeneity

Independence worked (above) with two variables in the same population. However, the question of homogeneity is whether different populations have the sample proportions of some characteristics?

The test of homogeneity differ from independence with its: motivation, sampling and hypotheses. Other than this it is works similarly. 

The example of male and female of the independence checks whether the gender and left-handedness
 are independent. Now we check if men and women have the same proportion of left-handedness.
  • H0: men and women have the same proportion of left-handedness
  • H1: men and women do not have the same proportion of left-handedness
  • moreover, the expected frequency is calculated by the prop. of the total number of ri (total ri over total; n) times the total number of c.
Generally: /H_0: the two populations have the same proportion of characteristics/ 
and /H_1: the two populations do not have the same proportion of characteristics/.

In the case of the example, we say: E11 = 240 * P(left-handedness) = 240 * 88/760. So Eij = (i-th row total)(j-th column total) / grand total

After checking the requirements (same of independence), we can compute the test statistic (same of independence).

2.3 Fisher's exact test

With this test we can test the two test discussed above (i.e. independence and homogeneity) when:

  1. the requirements of independence were not met.
    1. directed e.g. H0: row and columns are independent BUT H1: occurrence of "first column category" is more common than in group of "first row category.
  2. the requirements of homogeneity were not met.
    1. e.g. H0: two populations have the same proportion of one characteristics. BUT H1: the proportion of the characteristic is bigger in one population.

Moreover, we assume the number of rows and columns is fixed. And the marginal is hypergeometric distribution (just to compute the P-value), and the test statistic (frequencies in cell(1,1)) is always computed using R. Then we get the P-value which we compare against our α.
