1 Probability

Prob. is used extensively in statistics. For example, we want to conclude how what's the probability that a baby's birth is 21 early. Here we would have to gather samples (i.e. cases of different births) and using relative frequency measurement we can estimate the total special cases / total cases. Moreover, the more samples we have, the accurate probability we obtain.

  • Probability Experiment: produces different outcomes.
  • Sample space: a set of all possible outcomes, and it's denoted by Ω.
  • Event A, B,…: a collection of events; e.g. A={bbg, bgb, gbb}
  • Simple Event: this has only one expected outcome

1.1 Relative Frequency

This approach is used to approximate the probability: n of times A occurred / n of all trails. This approach is mainly used when it can be said that each outcome is equally likely to occur. E.g. Ronaldo scores 53 out of 60 penalties, We can then estimate the prob. he will score a penalty: 53/60 = .83

Moreover, larger data sets provide better estimates. This is supported by the law of large numbers. E.g. if you throw a die 100 times, you might get unstable outcomes (i.e. not as expected), however, it becomes more stable if we throw a thousand time.

1.2 Classical Approach

This works for theoretical models, i.e. each test has equally likely probability to occur. E.g. throwing a fair coin two times what's the prob. of getting 1 heads? we know the possible outcomes (23), then we know it's 2/8 = 1/4 = .25

1.3 Subjective approach

Happens when we have little information and the interpretation of an event depends on our subjective judgment.

2 Conditional Probability

In which the sample space is influenced by pas outcomes. E.g. if you roll a die twice and say "the first score was 3" what is the prob. the sum equals to 8? (i.e. the total sum after throwing the second die). After throwing a 3, you have 6 possibilities {(3,1), (3, 2)…(3, 6)} and to get a sum of 8, then we have on of these possibilities; i.e. (3,5) = 1/6. Thus, P(B|A) = 1/6. Pronounced as "B given A".

The general formula is: P(B|A) = P(A ∩ B) / P(A).

3 Addition Rule

Is when we have two events that could occur. E.g. we have two fair coins, and we want to know the prob. of throwing heads. A common mistake is to sum the two outcomes: 1/2 + 1/2 = 1. But this is not true. A solution is to write the smaple space: Ω = {HH, HT, TH, TT}, know we clearly see it's 3/4, and not 1.

Another possible solution, relates to the addition rule, is to sum the two possible outcomes AND subtract the prob. that both events occur: P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B). Translating that to the example would be: P(H) + P(H) - P(HH)

Here of course, the notation ∩ (intersection) and ∪ (union) is used to facilitate considering probabilities. Two events are disjoint, if they do not overlap wrt outcomes.

4 Multiplication Rule

Imagine we have a vase with 9 balls, and you draw two balls without returning the drawn ball back. What is the prob. of drawing 1 and then 2; P(1,2). The answer can be concluded in two ways: write the sample space and draw one ball, then write the sample space again after removing the first ball; we have only 8 balls left. So 1/81 is incorrect; because it's without replacement (i.e. we do not put the ball back).

Another solution is by means of the multiplication rule. P(1,2) = P(first 1) * P(ball 2 | ball 1 is drawn) = 1/9 * 1/8 = 1/72.

Note the multiplication rule is a rewritten formula of the conditional prob. 

CP: 
P(B|A) = P(A \cap B) / P(A).  

MR:
P(A \cap B) = P(A) * P(B | A)

4.1 Independent

Two events A and B are independent if: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) * P(B), where P(B) was substituted in P(A|B), which implies independence.

We can always check whether two events are independent using the formula.

4.2 Sampling methods

We have two methods:

  1. sampling with replacement; independent events: e.g. drawing ball 1 and replacing it back, does not influence the prob of drawing ball 3 in the second turn
  2. sampling without replacement; dependent events; e.g. drawing ball 1 and NOT replacing it back, does influence the prob. of drawing ball 3 in the second turn.

However, sometimes we treat a small sample from a large population we consider the events as independent ones. This is called small sample rule and it's to simplify calculations.

4.3 Complement rule

The complement of at least one (occurrence): P(>= 1 of A) = 1 - P( no occurrence of A).
