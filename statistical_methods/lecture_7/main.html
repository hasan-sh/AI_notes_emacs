1 Hypothesis Testing | Mean

Remember the steps of hypothesis testing:

  1. identify the population parameter
  2. formulate the H0 and H1 and the significance level.
    1. the H0 always has the = sign since this is the hypothetical value for which we are trying to find counter example.
    2. the H1, as said above, depends on the claim made.
  3. collect data
  4. make the assumption that H0 is true.
  5. choose test statistic and specify its distribution under H0.
  6. calculate the test statistic
  7. find the P-value: prob. that test statistic has more extreme values than the observed value
  8. based on 7 and α, make a conclusion regarding H0: reject (small P-value) or do not reject (large P-value).

The same idea for the population proportion applies for the population mean, however, here (i.e. for the mean) we either know σ or not:

1.1 σ known (very rare)

We calculate the test statistic based on the z score of the given sample mean:

  1. Z = (Xn - μ) / (σ/\sqrt(n)) note this is very similar to the test statistic of the population proportion, except the SD is different in this case.
  2. E.g. we have n=13 observations with sample mean X13 = 7.03. Further, we assume (e.g. from previous exams) the data is normally distributed. We also have σ = 1.93
    1. Our H0: μ = 6, and H1: μ != 6. Thus Z= (7.03 - 6) / (1.93/\sqrt(13)) ≈ 1.92.
    2. P-value: since it is a two sided test (since H1 != 6) and the test statistic is > 0, we calculate 2*P(Z>=1.92) = 2*(1-0.9726) = 0.0548.
      1. And 0.0548 > 0.05, thus we cannot reject the H0, meaning we do not have enough evidence that the mean value of the exam grades is (or is not) 6.
    3. Critical region: We can also find out the same result by the critical region method. Based on α, we know the extreme values are: -1.96 and 1.96, and z is neither < -1.96 nor > 1.96
    4. However, if we change H1: μ > 6, the P-value is P(Z>=1.92) ≈ 0.0274 which is < 0.05, thus we have enough evidence to reject the H0
      1. meaning the mean value of the exam grades is greater than 6.

1.2 σ unknown (more often):

We also construct a test statistic: T = (Xn - μ) / (Sn/sqrt(n)). 

The difference here is that we have S_n which is the SD of the sample

And this is a t-distribution. As the number of degrees of freedom increases (n-1 i.e. how many values can we decide, based on the mean, i.e. to obtain the mean (sum/n)), the t-distribution approaches a normal distribution.

To find the t-value (t(n-1,α)), we look at the table we can find: in the rows the degrees of freedom and the columns the values of the significance level.

Taking the same example above, with an exception that we do NOT know the σ, and H0: μ=6, H1: μ!=6, we also have S13 = 1.553 (calculated in R):

  1. T = (7.03-6) / 1.553 / sqrt(13) ≈ 2.393. And from the table:
    1. Since we have two sided tests and 12 DoF, we find the extreme T value as -2.179 and 2.179.
    2. The critical region is: -t12,0.025 and t12,0.025, and since t = 2.392 > 2.179, meaning it is in the critical region, we reject the H0.
      1. meaning we have enough evidence that the mean value of the exam grades is NOT 6.
when we have a t-distribution and n-1 DoF we can only use critical values (from tables at least). P-value can only be calculated in a software (e.g. using R).

Note that the E (i.e. margin of error) is equal to t(n-1, α/2) * Sn/sqrt(n) in the case of unknown σ. 

E.g. X_13 = 7.03, S_13 = 1.553, t_12,0.025 = 2.179: the confidence interval of 95% (i.e. 1-\alpha)
[7.03 - 2.179 * 1.553/sqrt(13), 7.03 + 2.179 * 1.553/sqrt(13)] = [6.092, 7.97]

2 Two samples problems

Is about making hypothesis testing between two samples. This happens when we, for instance, calculate the mean/proportion of two samples now and after doing a certain event. Then we can make a claim and do the hypothesis testing. There are two types:

  1. independent samples: the values of the pairs in the samples do NOT influence each other. In other words, there is no relationship between the samples. (e.g. two types of M&M have the same weight; the mean of one sample (i.e. one type) has no relation with the other sample).
    1. meaning samples do not apply as pairs.
    2. also, samples do not have to have the same size.
  2. dependent samples: if the values in one sample are related (or influence) the values of the other sample; e.g. diet A helps losing at least 10kg in 2 months (measuring the mean among individuals now and after two months is dependent).

Moreover, to calculate the difference, and consider the claims, between two samples, the n should be large enough or the data is of a normal distribution (then we find a test statistic).

2.1 defining hypothesis

the Null hypothesis would be defined as the difference between the parameters of the two samples (e.g. μ1 - μ2). Moreover, the alternative hypothesis depends on the H0:

  1. μ1 - μ2 < claimed value => left-tailed test
  2. μ1 - μ2 > claimed value => right-tailed test

  3. μ1 - μ2 != claimed value => two-sided test 

    (Dependent samples; matched pairs)
Claim:  certain task can be performed faster on a bigger screen

Experiment: 60 randomly selected students performed the same task on a 21” and a 42” screenData:  completion times (sec.)
With \alpha 0.10.

Our H_0: \mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0 AND H_1: \mu_1 > \mu_2 # meaning who performed with first screen needed more time!

And since we have n > 30, we can use the T_d tset with t_59 (i.e. DoF). We know D = 0.286

Now we apply in the same forumla, but using D as the parameter, and H_0 as the mean:
t_d = (0.286 - 0) / 4.921 / sqrt(60) \approx 0.4502 # where 4.921 is the SD of the differences

From the t-dist table we have: t_(59, 0.1) = 1.296, thus 0.4502 is not in the critical region (i.e. not < -1.296 nor >1.296). 
We can say we have no enough evidence that bigger screens are better.

(D calculated by the average of the differences; i.e. X_1-Y_1, X_n-Y_n)

Finally, we can also define the confidence interval of, for example, 90%, by:

E = t(n-1,α/2) * Sd/sqrt(n) = 1.671 *4.921/sqrt(60) ≈ 1.06. Thus: [0.286-1.06, 0.286+1.06] ≈ [-0.78, 1.35] 

1.671 is extracted from the t-dist table under 59 DoF and 0.1/2 significance level.

Since the confidence interval has a negative value, it is enough evidence to say we cannot conclude that bigger screen are better.
