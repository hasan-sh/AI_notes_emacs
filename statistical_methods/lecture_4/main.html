1 Standard normal distribution

1.1 continuous random variable

is a variable that as an uncountable number of values. We can calculate its prob. distribution by the density function. These probabilities are computed by the area.

Moreover, when X is a continuous random variable, we do not assign it to a specific value (e.g. 1) and calculate its prob., but we consider an interval in which X can be evaluated (which is most likely to be infinite). Then the prob. distribution is the area under the curve (basic calculation: height*width)

Notice the area (for this type of distribution) is expected to be 1. And P(X) is always >= 0.

A bell-shaped graph is always symmetric and indicates a normal distribution (i.e. area under the curve).

1.2 compute normal distribution

  • P(X <= z) = area under density to the left of z
  • P(a <= X <= b) = P(X<=b) - P(X<=a)
  • P(X>=b) = 1 - P(X<=b)

Moreover, to find the z value, we have to look at the table for the for the value that results from: z = (X-μ)/σ

2 Central Limit Theorem

CLT is the sample mean (average) of the distribution among n samples. If n > 30, CLT applies for any distribution, however, if it is less, it only applies for a distribution that's normally distributed.

The shape of the graph suggests normality: e.g. bell-shaped.

Normally, we have a continuous variable, we can compute its prob. as in interval: e.g. X >= 3 and <=6. This works by applying these formulas and then extracting the actual prob from the table. However, when we want to compute the distribution of an n sample, we would use the CLT formula (although this computation can be done manually, it is tedious to do so): N(μ, σ/n) and subsequently, we calculate z = (X-μ) / \sqrt(σ/n). Notice the formula does not differ much from the one for calculating a single test X, but only we divide by n.

3 Assessing normality

  1. We can check the shape of the graph (e.g. histogram).
    1. However, t-distribution has a very similar shape, but it's not a normal distribution.
  2. better use: QQ-plot: When points lie on a straight line, then we find a normal distribution.

QQ-plot: is a quantile-quantile plot. Meaning we have quantile from the data set, and the second quantile is of the theoretical data (i.e. z).

Moreover, we have three types of QQ-plots:

  1. x-axis has the theoretical quantiles, and y-axis the sample quantiles -> to assess the type of distribution
  2. x-axis theoretical quantiles of prob. distribution, and y-axis theoretical quantiles of another prob. distribution, -> to compare the shape of two prob. distributions (eg check for location-scale family).
  3. x-axis sample quantiles of a dataset, y-axis sample quantiles of another one -> to compare the shape of the two data distributions and assess whether they originate from models that belong to the same location-scale family
